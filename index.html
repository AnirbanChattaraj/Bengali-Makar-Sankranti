Bengali Makar Sankranti is not just a harvest festival — it is a sacred farewell to Poush month, a celebration of abundance, devotion, sweets, and centuries-old rural traditions.

Explore the Festival

What is Bengali Makar Sankranti?

In Bengal, Makar Sankranti is known as Poush Sankranti. Unlike many Indian regions, Bengalis focus more on the end of the month of Poush rather than the solar transition alone. It marks the arrival of longer days, agricultural prosperity, and deep spiritual reflection.

Fact: Traditional Bengali calendars consider Poush Sankranti more culturally significant than Magh Sankranti.

Sacred Rituals & Traditions

Snan & Daan

Devotees wake up before sunrise to take a holy bath in rivers like the Ganga, Damodar, or local ponds. Donating rice, clothes, and sweets is believed to purify the soul.

Rooted in ancient Hindu solar worship practices.

Poush Mela

Inspired by Rabindranath Tagore, Poush Mela at Shantiniketan celebrates folk music, Baul songs, handicrafts, and rural Bengal’s soul.

Started in 1894 as a spiritual-cultural gathering.

Til & Nolen Gur

Winter delicacies made from sesame seeds and date palm jaggery are offered to gods and shared with neighbors as symbols of unity.

Nolen Gur is harvested only in winter nights.

The Legendary Bengali Sweets

Pithe Puli

Handmade rice cakes filled with coconut and jaggery, steamed or fried, passed down through generations of Bengali households.

Patishapta

Thin crepes filled with kheer, coconut, or jaggery — traditionally prepared by elders during winter evenings.

Joynagarer Moa

A GI-tagged sweet made of khoi and nolen gur from South 24 Parganas, symbolizing regional pride.

Stories & Beliefs

Folklore says Goddess Lakshmi visits homes during Poush Sankranti to bless hardworking families. That is why houses are cleaned, alpana is drawn, and new rice is cooked with devotion.

In rural Bengal, unmarried girls prepare pithe believing it brings prosperity and good fortune.
